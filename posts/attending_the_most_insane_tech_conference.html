I recently attended a tech conference in LA because one of my favorite podcasts, the All-In podcast. I got to hear live debates, discussions of the future of AI, and most interestingly to me, AI energy infrastructure.

I prompted ChatGPT the following:

Give me a positive review of the All-In summit, similar to a positive review that you would see rating the food, speakers, networking, and overall educational quality of the event. I met so many amazing founders and builders from all over the world, with many of them leveraging AI agents for very specific markets. To give me a proper review, look at the all in summit YouTube channel and X posts (scrape the web) on the main quotes of the summit and biggest takeaways. Try to make it more of a blog post with structured paragraphs and transitions, and make sure to explain the summit in a way that people from non tech backgrounds can understand. for example, try to define everything within the sentences like a quick “Alex Karp, CEO of Palantir”, etc. Also include that at the end they rented out all of universal studios for the attendees.

This was the response I recieved:

what is the all-in summit?:

The All-In Summit is part conference, part cultural moment. It’s hosted by the team behind the All-In Podcast (Chamath Palihapitiya, Jason Calacanis, David Sacks, and David Friedberg) and brings together founders, builders, investors, policymakers, and thought leaders to talk about where technology is going — with a special focus this year on artificial intelligence (AI). For anyone not from a tech background, think of it as a mix between TED-style talks, policy debates, and startup networking, all compressed into a few high-energy days.

my experience: ideas + people:

When I arrived, I expected vision and hype. What I found was something more valuable: grounded conversations with people who are actually building. Many of the attendees I met were leveraging AI “agents” — software systems that can perform tasks on their own, like scheduling, customer support, or healthcare documentation — for very specific industries. These weren’t abstract pitches; they were early products already in pilot programs across fields as diverse as energy, finance, and medical operations.

speakers & talks:

The lineup was stacked and surprisingly approachable. Elon Musk (CEO of Tesla and SpaceX) explored how robotics and satellite networks will reshape not just industries but entire countries. Alex Karp (CEO of Palantir) delivered a sweeping talk on geopolitics and the need for software that can operate in mission-critical environments. Adena Friedman (CEO of Nasdaq) connected market structure and capital flows to the realities of AI deployment. And one of the most memorable sessions was a debate between Mark Cuban (entrepreneur and investor) and Tucker Carlson (media personality), which surfaced the very real policy tradeoffs between innovation, regulation, and speech. For people not steeped in AI, the speakers made sure to define concepts clearly — from what “agentic workflows” mean in practice (automated systems handling tasks end-to-end) to why regulation isn’t just a buzzword but a requirement for adoption in fields like healthcare and defense.

networking & atmosphere:

The density of talent was unlike any other event I’ve attended. Between talks, I found myself sketching out go-to-market strategies with a founder from Europe, brainstorming workflow integrations with a health-tech builder from New York, and trading notes with an investor from Asia. It wasn’t about prestige or photo-ops — it was about real conversations, grounded in real work. The logistics were smooth, the food stations kept people energized, and the atmosphere struck a rare balance: ambitious yet welcoming.

educational value:

What I appreciated most was how the summit balanced inspiration with accessibility. Even if you didn’t have a technical background, you could walk away understanding the major tradeoffs in AI: how good data beats fancy models, why distribution matters more than novelty, and how regulation and infrastructure are evolving together.

the grand finale: universal studios

And then, as if the content wasn’t enough, the organizers pulled off something unforgettable: on the final night, they rented out all of Universal Studios just for attendees. Imagine wrapping up a day of deep discussions on the future of AI, then riding roller coasters with the same founders, investors, and builders you’d been brainstorming with hours earlier. It was the perfect mix of work and play — and a reminder that this community is about building serious things while still having fun along the way.

final thoughts:

If I had to sum it up: the All-In Summit is a 4.8/5 experience. The speakers were world-class, the networking was unparalleled, the logistics ran smoothly, and the closing night was pure magic. More than anything, I left with new frameworks for thinking about AI, new friends across industries, and a renewed sense that the future isn’t being imagined — it’s already being built.
